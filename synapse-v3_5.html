A 68-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion and bilateral leg swelling. Echocardiography shows EF of 35%. Which of the following is the primary pathophysiological mechanism?
A Increased afterload from systemic vasoconstriction
B Reduced myocardial contractility leading to decreased stroke volume
C Diastolic dysfunction with impaired ventricular relaxation
D Right ventricular failure secondary to pulmonary hypertension
Why B is correct
An EF of 35% (<40%) confirms systolic heart failure. The hallmark is reduced contractility → decreased stroke volume → compensatory neurohormonal activation. This matches the clinical picture of HFrEF.

Question
What is the primary difference between HFrEF and HFpEF in terms of ejection fraction and underlying mechanism?
📄 Harrison's Ch.12 — Page 47
Answer
  • • HFrEF: EF <40%, caused by reduced myocardial contractility (systolic dysfunction)
  • • HFpEF: EF ≥50%, caused by impaired ventricular relaxation (diastolic dysfunction)
  • • HFrEF responds to ACEi/ARB + beta-blockers + spironolactone

🫀 Heart Failure Classification
Heart failure is classified by ejection fraction: HFrEF (<40%) is associated with systolic dysfunction and responds well to neurohormonal blockade. HFpEF (≥50%) involves diastolic dysfunction and has fewer evidence-based therapies...
💊 High-Yield Pharmacology
ACE inhibitors reduce mortality in HFrEF. Beta-blockers (carvedilol, metoprolol) reduce sudden cardiac death risk. Spironolactone indicated when EF <35%...
Key takeaways
EF <40% = systolic HF; always check eligibility for ACEi + BB
Loop diuretics for symptom relief but do NOT improve mortality
NT-proBNP >900 pg/mL is diagnostic threshold in acute setting
